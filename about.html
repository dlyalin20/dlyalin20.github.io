What is Ethics Bowl?


Put simply, Ethics Bowl is a competition between two teams on a variety of social, political, religious and environmental dilemmas. Teams will present their stance or comment on the moral permissibility of certain acts, often drawing from philosophical considerations and research. Aside from the competitive aspect, Ethics Bowl uses a unique format and has a different objective from usual highschool debate. Instead of having a rigid, pre-determined stance for participants, the competition facilitates a structured discussion that underscores the presentation of premises, important points and commentary that challenges those points. Instead of disagreeing just for the sake of it, the team that exhibits more precision and presents more complex talking points will win, even if their stances are similar.

Why is Ethics Bowl’s approach more useful than regular debate?


Whether it’s heated debates or partisan media, divisive politics plagues most issues, and its not always the most productive manner of coming to solutions. While debate is advantageous for strengthening rhetorical skills, Ethics Bowl accomplishes this while also being an effective model for resolving real-world issues. The focal point is to work together to accurately determine the premises and morally salient points relavent to a particular issue. Instead of verbally sparring with cherry picked data, all mindsets are considered and moral delineations justified. We make room for agreement and common ground when possible and try to bring nuance to the discussion.

What is the Ethics Bowl format?


The Regional and National Ethics Bowl rules are subject to change. The most recent format is found on page 4 of the official rulebook. The general structure is that two teams will have time to discuss two different cases within one round. For the first case, one team will have time to confer and present, and the other team will have time to confer and give commentary. The first team will have time to confer and respond. During the discussion of the next case, roles between teams are switched, so both teams will have the same amount of speaking time overall. At the end of a round, judges will score each team based on delivery, rhetoric and other criteria.

What are Ethics Bowl cases?


Ethics bowl releases a packet of 15-17 cases, which presents a curated selection of hypothetical scenarios and general real world issues. The regional competition occurs at the beginning of each year and packets are released a few months in advance. The national competition is open to qualified teams who won their regional competitions during the spring of each year, and the packet is released 4-5 weeks in advance for participants to study. Examples of these cases can be found in the official case archive.



The Ethics Bowl Team at Stuyvesant

Our team not only aims to compete at the regional and national levels each year, but to get more people to reflect on and incorporate ethical considerations in their lives. Whether they have a personal conflict to resolve, whether they are the decision-makers of another club or organization, or whether they are coming to a stance on a national or world issue, ethics, rhetorical skills, and abilities to fairly resolve issues gained through participating in Ethics Forum are always going to be relevant and integral.

We plan on integrating with the rest of the student body at our very own Stuyvesant High School through a digital magazine, Ethical Inquiries (and Where to Find Them), which is open to art and writing submissions from general students regarding ethics topics.

We also have plans to host events with panel discussions annually to get students engaged in ethics, thinking more deeply about controversial issues, and getting more exposure to professional discussions, with the hope that they will model such professionalism in other areas of their lives and careers.
